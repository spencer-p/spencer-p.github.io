The Cake Pizza

Dec 26 2020 — 4 minute read

One of the most memorable pizzas I had was a giant, inches-tall dutch-oven shaped piece of bread with a more conventional pizza resting on top. I probably liked it so much because it was served in the cold forest at night after a long hike, but that won’t stop me from trying to recreate this ultimate sicilian pizza.

Attempt #1

I don’t have a dutch oven in the required size, and I am additionally afraid of making something too accurate and disliking it. Instead, I will make it in a deep casserole dish, a sort of beefed-up focaccia if you will.

I want high hydration for a thick bread-y pizza. The toppings are not as complicated as the dough.

The recipe

For the dough:

  • 75% hydration
  • 2% salt
  • 1.5% yeast
  • 2-3% oil
    • Oil is key to depth of flavor for a dough-heavy pizza.
  • 2-3% sugar
    • Sugar is a unique feature of American pizza dough. I am not sure if I would like with or without sugar.

This puts us at about 182% of the weight of the flour. I don’t know exactly how much dough is needed, so I’ll be working backwards from the amount of flour that fits in half of my baking dish.

For the topping:

  • Canned tomato. Crushed, whole, or sauce, depending on desired effort.
  • Mozzarella block.
  • Fresh basil

The method will be to mix and prepare the dough a day in advance. When the day comes, I will warm the dough at room temperature until risen in the casserole dish. I will top the pizza and bake it for 25 minutes at 500°F, or until the internal temperature reaches 190°F.

The first result

I used 500g of bread flour and about a cup each of sauce and cheese.

The good: The pizza was a good one to two inches tall and satisfied my desire for bready pizza fresh out of the oven.

The bad: I overcooked the pizza. The dough was internally 210°F and the cheese was burnt in some places. The cheese being burnt is not so bad, but dry dough is. The pizza stuck to the casserole dish - this could be a combination of not enough oil as well as overcooking.

The ugly: The dough had difficulty supporting the weight of the sauce and cheese. Sauce pressed down and made pockets in the dough.

The topping pressure suggests there is a maximum height to this pizza without changing the recipe. You either need to sacrifice fluffiness in the dough and make it dense, or pre-bake the dough and add the topping once the dough is set. In my next attempt, I will try the blind baking method.

Attempt #2

I’ll use the same method as before, with the following modifications:

  • More liberal oil on the casserole dish
  • Cut the baking time down to 20 minutes total
  • First ten minutes will be just the dough
  • Add the toppings halfway through, then finish for ten minutes

After 20 minutes, I will test the temperature and make sure it gets up to 190°F internally.

I predict this method will result in a taller pizza with a more consistently-baked dough and a fresh layer of topping.

The second result

As predicted, the modified recipe resulted in a more even bake throughout the dough that stood up to the weight of the sauce and cheese. It cooked evenly, had a crust without burning, and was moist and fluffy. It was not significantly taller than the previous iteration, but the more sturdy dough structure made it feel as though it was.

While the first attempt had an awkward gradient from dough to cheese, this second attempt was clearly a piece of bread with a pizza on top. (Yes, that’s a good thing.)

Conclusion

The pizza I made does not compare to the monstrous pizza of my memory. But it is delightful, filling, and clearly what it was meant to be: an overgrown sicilian pizza, a loaf of bread with tomato and cheese.

I love it.
